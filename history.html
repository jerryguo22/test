What The Overlay Suggests

Redlined areas remain socially distinct. C and D HOLC areas have much higher people-of-color shares than A areas in the contemporary tract overlay.
D areas show the highest average exposure. Average allergen exposure rises from A/B/C to D in the tract-centroid overlay.
Housing tenure matters. Renter share is highest in D areas, matching the separate correlation result where renter share is the second strongest socioeconomic signal.

This page frames a historical hypothesis: allergy exposure should be studied as an environmental equity issue shaped by historical urban space. It does not claim redlining alone caused present-day pollen or air-quality patterns.
